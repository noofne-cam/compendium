- **Coordinates:** $(q_i) = \set{r, \theta, \phi}$ (Spherical Polar Coordinates). As $$\begin{align}x &= q_1\sin q_2\cos q_3\\y &= q_1\sin q_2\sin q_3 \\ z &= q_1\cos q_2\end{align}$$we have $$\begin{align}\dot x^2 &= q_1^2(\dot q_2 \cos q_2\cos q_3 - \dot q_3 \sin q_2 \sin q_2)^2\\ \dot y^2 &= q_1^2(\dot q_2\cos q_2 \sin q_3 + \dot q_3 \sin q_2 \cos q_3)^2 \\ \dot z^2 &= q_1^2\dot q_2^2 \sin^2 q_2\end{align},$$whence $$\begin{align}v^2 &= \dot x^2 + \dot y^2 + \dot z^2\\ &= q_1^2[\dot q_2^2(\cos^2 q_2\cos^2 q_3 + \cos^2 q_2 \sin^2 q_3) + \dot q_3^2(\sin^2 q_2 \sin^2 q_3 + \sin^2 q_2 \cos^2 q_3)] \\ &+ q_1^2\dot q_2^2 \sin^2q_2\\ &= q_1^2[\dot q_2^2\cos^2 q_2 + \dot q_3^2\sin^2 q_2 + \dot q_2^2 \sin^2 q_2]\\ &= q_1^2(\dot q_3^2\sin^2 q_2 + \dot q_2^2)\end{align},$$from which it is easily seen that $q_1 = l$ is fixed. Therefore, we may write $$T = \frac{1}{2}ml^2(\dot q_3^2\sin^2 q_2 + \dot q_2^2).$$From a geometrical pondering it follows that $$U = mgz = mgl \cos q_2.$$
- **Lagrangian.** The two equations above combine to form $$\mathcal L = \frac{1}{2}ml^2(\dot q_3^2 \sin^2 q_2 + \dot q_2^2) - mgl\cos q_2.$$Thus the azimuthal coordinate is cyclic. The Euler-Lagrange equation for the polar coordinate $q_2$ becomes $$\frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial q_2} = \frac{d}{dt}\left( \frac{\partial \mathcal L}{\partial \dot q_2} \right) \implies ml^2 \ddot q_2 = -mgl\sin q_2 + ml^2\sin q_2 \cos q_2$$and that of the azimuthal $q_3$ $$\frac{d}{dt}(ml^2\dot q_3\sin^2 q_2) = 0.$$
- **Equations of Motion.** $$\boxed{\begin{align}\ddot q_2 &= -\frac{g}{l}\sin q_2 + \frac{1}{2} \dot q_3^2 \sin 2q_2 \\ \ddot q_3 &= -\frac{2\dot q_2 \dot q_3 \cos q_2}{\sin q_2}\end{align}}$$
